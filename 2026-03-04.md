### Electric Monks

One detour this week was an experiment with Kyle Mathews' [Electric Monks](https://github.com/KyleAMathews/hegelian-dialectic-skill) Claude skill. It takes a question or tension, and launches subagents to argue for different positions related to the question. Then another subagent synthesizes them, tests them, and runs a new round, for three or four rounds in total.

This skill is in the same family of interfaces that I've been investigating: multi-user (ish) agent interfaces that simulate different perspectives and weave them together, that use AI to alternate between diverging and converging, and implement the [design double diamond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_Diamond_(design_process_model)).

I ran a full dialectic yesterday, with some manual intervention to push the AI towards high-quality lines of thought. Here's a visualization that [conundrumer](https://x.com/conundrumer) generated for the entire process:

![pasted-20260304-230334-kinc3x.png](./.assets/pasted-20260304-230334-kinc3x.png)

The initial question: Why hasn't there been more progress towards using AI for governance?

The dialectical pairs (note that none of these tensions try to answer the question directly):

- Should AI be a mirror that reflects collective beliefs back to groups, or a mediator that generates bridging proposals and facilitates deliberation?
- Is the goal of deploying AI to improve epistemics and get the facts right, or is governance about politics, not epistemics?
- Do we have to design carefully, or build first and learn through practice?

The synthesis after three rounds basically arrived at the concept of _practice design_: if we want to use AI for governance in a way that's impactful, we should be leading by working with communities, to design forms of use that bring people together to deploy it.

Is this an good answer? The quality of the process is more important than the right answer. But as a first sign, it _does_ roughly match the state-of-the-art[^1] in how people in the field think about deploying AI.

I did find the process to be valuable:

- The dialectic did an excellent job of covering relevant subject matter. Our subagents surveyed about 30 distinct examples including relevant economics, philosophers, research projects, and other case studies.
- They tested and re-tested arguments, and actually bailed in places where it became clear that tensions weren't germane to a productive synthesis.
- There were several promising points in the middle of the dialectic where we could have branched into a productive sidebar based on the intermediate output alone. (This is something we'll explore in our interface designs later.)

Some challenges:

- The quality of the *raw* AI generated text for the monks' positions was questionable. This seemed to be more of a style problem than a content problem, since the summaries were fine. A reason could have been aggressive prompting from the skill that encouraged the monks to take strong opinions.
- I was fairly neutral towards the choice of debates that were staged in the dialectical steps; it was a surprise to get a final synthesis that seemed coherent and on-point.
- Was the dialectic just reflecting back the user driving the system? In the end, I wasn't too worried about that, since the steering I had to do was minimally related to the conclusion.

I think further investigation would largely verify that the model was doing less of echoing my own inputs back to me, and doing more of constructing a coherent, robust frame using loose hints/constraints. That's what we want!

I also left the experiment confident that if we had more people involved in the dialectics, it would have captured their views as well.
