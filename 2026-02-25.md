### February 25, 2026

Rewrote Braid over the last two days, to transform it from a knowledge base and agentic chat into something that looks more like a question-and-answer site. I also tuned a set of prompts to generate short summaries for a feed:

![pasted-20260225-144003-6bglc1.png](.input/documents/.assets/pasted-20260225-144003-6bglc1.png)

This iteration is the first one where I'm starting to consider what the platform feels like. At this point, I'm not getting much signal on whether this is useful yet (the _content_ could be compelling? hard to say).

A few issues: AI responses are too long, people already have strong habit paths around Claude/ChatGPT, the question density is too high, etc.

But I think the main problem is that the site doesn't feel right. Once people had positive associations with Quora-like websites, when Quora was high quality, but that basically ended in the early 2010s. Medium adaptation means that unprimed, neutral users will come to a piece of software with strong preconceptions, and currently those associations are against things that look like "feeds" or "platforms". This isn't even a memes or a community problem, it's just a problem with how the design feels.

I'm gonna fall back to the only known way to address this, which is borrowing heavily from an existing design language where people already have strong associations for this kind of information exchange.

Bluesky did this well - I didn't give them enough credit at the time, but many people remarked about how it felt exactly like classic Twitter. They rolled back the design language just enough to match Twitter from about 2-3 years earlier, so no early Twitter jankiness, but people knew exactly how to use the application when it launched.

Looking back, I also did see this strategy work twice before... once for a forum in college where we had a natural A/B test because it forked. The competing forum changed the design language because the dev wanted to rewrite everything in Ruby on Rails and everyone was confused, and went to the version of the forum that looked familiar.

The second is from working on DAOs: there was a period of borrowing heavily from Snapshot's design language. Snapshot was a widely used governance tool that served a very different niche at the time. I haven't thought about it since writing this note, but for the period it existed, I think that was one of the critical decisions that made people understand how to use our thing.

At the current pace, it'll take about a day and a half to implement a new design language. Part of that design language will be condensing the length of summary responses shown to users.

After that, I plan to take a day to scaffold some information architecture, e.g. boards, tags, social graph - which should make the barebones prototype above feel more substantial. And then we will know if there's something here.
